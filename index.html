### MSc Short Notes: Biodiversity

Definition

Biodiversity refers to the variety of life forms on Earth, including diversity within species (genetic diversity), between species (species diversity), and of ecosystems (ecosystem diversity).

Levels of Biodiversity


1. Genetic Diversity

- Variation of genes within species.
- Essential for adaptability and survival.

2. Species Diversity

- Variety of species within a habitat or region.
- Measured by species richness (number of species) and evenness (distribution).

3. Ecosystem Diversity

- Diversity of habitats, ecosystems, and ecological processes.
- Includes forests, wetlands, grasslands, coral reefs, etc.

Importance of Biodiversity


1. Ecological Balance

- Maintains ecosystem services like nutrient cycling, pollination, and soil formation.

2. Economic Value

- Provides resources such as food, medicine, and raw materials.

3. Cultural Value

- Supports recreation, tourism, and traditional knowledge.

4. Resilience to Change

- Increases ecosystem stability against climate change and natural disasters.

Threats to Biodiversity


1. Habitat Loss

- Deforestation, urbanization, and agriculture.

2. Pollution

- Air, water, and soil contamination affecting species survival.

3. Climate Change

- Alters habitats, leading to species migration or extinction.

4. Overexploitation

- Unsustainable hunting, fishing, and resource extraction.

5. Invasive Species

- Non-native species disrupting local ecosystems.

Conservation of Biodiversity


1. In-situ Conservation

- Protecting species in their natural habitats.
Examples: National parks, wildlife sanctuaries, biosphere reserves.

2. Ex-situ Conservation

- Conserving species outside their natural habitats.
Examples: Zoos, botanical gardens, seed banks.

3. Legal Measures

- International Efforts: Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD), CITES.
- National Efforts: Wildlife Protection Act (India, 1972), Environment Protection Act (1986).

4. Sustainable Practices

- Promoting eco-friendly agriculture, forestry, and tourism.

Hotspots of Biodiversity


1. Criteria:

- Must have at least 1,500 species of vascular plants as endemics.
- Should have lost at least 70% of its original habitat.

2. Examples:

- Amazon Rainforest, Sundaland, Himalayas, Indo-Burma region.

Biodiversity and Ecosystem Services


1. Provisioning Services

- Food, water, timber, fiber.

2. Regulating Services

- Climate regulation, disease control, pollination.

3. Cultural Services

- Spiritual, recreational, educational.

4. Supporting Services

- Soil formation, nutrient cycling.




### Inhenced best notes: Biodiversity


Definition

Biodiversity refers to the variety of life on Earth, including the diversity within species, between species, and of ecosystems. It is the foundation of life and ecological balance on the planet.

Types of Biodiversity


1. Genetic Diversity

- Variability in genes within a single species.
- Example: Different rice varieties or breeds of cattle.

2. Species Diversity

- Variety of species within a habitat or region.
- Example: Coral reefs host thousands of marine species.

3. Ecosystem Diversity

- Variety of ecosystems in a given area.
- Example: Grasslands, wetlands, and forests.

Importance of Biodiversity



Ecological Balance

- Supports ecosystem stability and resilience.
- Pollination, nutrient cycling, and water purification.

Economic Value

- Food and Agriculture: Provides diverse crops and livestock.
- Medicines: 70% of medicines are derived from natural sources.
- Industries: Timber, biofuel, and fisheries.

Cultural and Recreational Value

- Tourism and cultural heritage.
- Spiritual and aesthetic significance.

Climate Resilience

- Forests and wetlands act as carbon sinks, mitigating climate change.

Threats to Biodiversity



1. Habitat Loss and Fragmentation

- Due to deforestation, agriculture, and urbanization.
- Example: Loss of tropical rainforests.

2. Climate Change

- Rising temperatures and extreme weather disrupt ecosystems.
- Example: Coral bleaching due to warming oceans.

3. Pollution

- Contaminates air, water, and soil.
- Example: Oil spills harming marine biodiversity.

4. Overexploitation

- Unsustainable hunting, fishing, and logging.
- Example: Overfishing causing population decline in marine species.

5. Invasive Species

- Non-native species that outcompete local flora and fauna.
- Example: Water hyacinth in freshwater ecosystems.

Conservation of Biodiversity



1. In-situ Conservation

- Protecting biodiversity in its natural habitat.
- Examples:
- National Parks: Gir National Park (Asiatic lions).
- Wildlife Sanctuaries: Blackbuck Sanctuary, Velavadar.
- Biosphere Reserves: Nilgiri Biosphere Reserve.

2. Ex-situ Conservation

- Conservation of species outside their natural habitats.
- Examples:
- Seed banks (National Seed Bank).
- Zoos and botanical gardens.

3. Legal and Policy Frameworks

- International:
- Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD).
- Ramsar Convention on Wetlands.
- CITES (Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species).
- National (India):
- Wildlife Protection Act (1972).
- Environment Protection Act (1986).
- National Biodiversity Authority (2003).

4. Community Participation

- Involving local communities for sustainable resource management.
- Example: Joint Forest Management (JFM).

5. Sustainable Practices

- Eco-friendly farming and forestry methods.
- Promotion of renewable energy.

Biodiversity Hotspots



Definition

Hotspots are regions with high levels of species richness and endemism under threat of habitat loss.

Criteria

1. At least 1,500 species of vascular plants as endemics.
2. At least 70% of the original habitat lost.

Global Hotspots

1. Amazon Rainforest.
2. Indo-Burma Region.
3. Sundaland (Indonesia).

Indian Hotspots

1. Himalayas: Includes northeastern states.
2. Western Ghats: Rich in endemic plant and animal species.
3. Indo-Burma: Encompasses northeastern India and Myanmar.
4. Sundaland: Includes Andaman and Nicobar Islands.

Conservation Strategies in India



1. Project Tiger

- Launched in 1973 for tiger conservation.
- Example: Ranthambore Tiger Reserve.

2. Project Elephant

- Aims to protect elephants and their habitats.
- Example: Kaziranga National Park.

3. Biodiversity Act, 2002

- Promotes conservation and sustainable use of biological resources.
- Establishes National Biodiversity Authority.

4. Ramsar Sites

- Wetlands of international importance.
- Example: Chilika Lake (Odisha), Sambhar Lake (Rajasthan).

Global Conservation Efforts



1. IUCN Red List

- Tracks the conservation status of species globally.

2. Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD)

- Signed at the Earth Summit, 1992.
- Focus on conservation, sustainable use, and equitable benefit-sharing.

3. United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs)

- Goal 14: Life below water.
- Goal 15: Life on land.
